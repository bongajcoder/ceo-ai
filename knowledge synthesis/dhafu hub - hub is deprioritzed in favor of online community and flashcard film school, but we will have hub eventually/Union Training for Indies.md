## 1. Partner with Unions and Industry Training Programs

**Leverage existing union training programs by forming official partnerships.** Many safety and leadership courses are run by industry unions and trusts like IATSE (International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees), Local 600 (International Cinematographers Guild), and CSATF (Contract Services Administration Trust Fund). - **Open a dialogue with union training departments:** Contact the training division of IATSE or Local 600 and express your goals. Explain that you want to **offer their safety or leadership courses to independent filmmakers** who are not on union jobs. Emphasize shared values – safer sets benefit the whole industry. Ask about outreach or **community access programs**. For example, IATSE’s Training Trust Fund (TTF) provides courses to help crew maintain skills with evolving technology, including online “Safety First!” classes【1†L60-L67】. - **Invite union instructors for special workshops:** Even if a full partnership is not feasible initially, ask if **union safety officers or experienced guild members** can lead standalone workshops at your space. ## 3. Ensure Training is Recognized or Certified (Even Outside Official Channels)

A key concern is whether these trainings “count” for anything. **Can independent filmmakers get certifications that are recognized by the industry, even if taken outside union-sanctioned channels?** The honest answer: *official union recognition* is challenging to obtain unless the training is done through the union or CSATF system. However, there are ways to **maximize credibility** of the training you provide:

- **Understand union certification limitations:** The CSATF Safety Pass courses, for example, are tracked in a central database for union and rostered crew. If a person isn’t in that system, taking a duplicate course elsewhere won’t automatically insert them into the official record. In practice, if a crew member later joins IATSE or works on a union project, **they will likely need to retake the official Safety Pass courses** to be in compliance, unless they had some prior arrangement. *However, that doesn’t mean outside training is wasted!* It equips them to pass those courses easily and work safely in the meantime. Make this clear to participants: your training will give them knowledge and perhaps a certificate of completion, but not a union “card” or roster listing.

- **Pursue recognition through affiliated groups:** One way to get a form of recognition is via organizations like the Producers Guild or industry associations:
  - The **PGA, for instance, has arranged special deals for its members to take safety courses via Contract Services**【15†L247-L254】. If any of your space’s leaders or frequent producers are PGA members, they could leverage those deals so that at least *they* get officially certified training. They can then lead by example on set.
  - Some **film schools and associations can access union trainings**. The PGA notes that they conduct harassment workshops for “members of guilds, unions, **film schools and other associations**”【15†L255-L261】. If your coworking space has or can form a nonprofit “association” of independent filmmakers, you might be able to **join such multi-organization training events** and get a certificate of attendance.

- **Create your own certificate of completion:** For the training sessions you run in-house (whether using third-party content or your own curriculum), provide attendees with a nicely formatted **certificate or digital badge**. While not officially sanctioned, it can list the topics covered (e.g. “Completed 10-hour On-Set Safety & Leadership Training – covering general safety, equipment handling, harassment prevention, and inclusive leadership”). This at least gives filmmakers something to show. The nonprofit MediaMKRS, for example, issues **digital badges** for skills learned in their programs【7†L86-L94】. You can do similarly. Platforms like Credly or Badgr let you create digital badges for competencies. If your program gains a good reputation, local producers might start recognizing your badge as a mark of a well-trained crew member.

- **Leverage the “Prior-Experienced Individual” route for union recognition:** If any of your trainees do get hired on a union project via the diversity pipeline, they will go through official training then. Under the new **PEI program in the IATSE Basic Agreement, non-roster new hires from underrepresented groups must complete CSATF training before starting the union job【10†L101-L109】**. The producer has to request training access for them【10†L107-L114】. If your coworking space’s program successfully prepares someone for that opportunity, you can guide them to alert the producer of this requirement. While *you* can’t certify them, you can help them navigate getting the official training when the time comes. Essentially, your training becomes a springboard – they’ll have the knowledge base to ace any union-led sessions and tests.

- **Emphasize compliance with standards over “official” labels:** Remind independent producers using your space that even if a crew member’s safety course isn’t union-certified, what matters most on a non-union shoot is *knowledge and compliance*. You’re implementing the **same OSHA and industry standards** as the unions. For example, your camera operators will know to wear PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) and follow proper rigging safety, your sets will adhere to the published Safety Bulletins (which are identical whether it’s union or not). You can even keep **documentation of the training content** in your production files. If an insurance company or stakeholder asks “Have your crew been trained in safety?” you can show the curriculum and certificates from your program. This may not be a universally recognized credential, but it demonstrates due diligence. In some cases, insurers and financiers are satisfied to see that a production conducted safety briefings or training sessions internally if union courses weren’t available.

In summary, **formal recognition by unions will remain limited** for outside training, but **practical recognition** – by way of safer sets, more hireable crew, and preparedness for eventual union work – is absolutely achievable. To bolster this, continue seeking any chance to piggyback on official programs (as noted, PGA or film commission initiatives) so your folks get as many “seals of approval” as possible.

## 4. Handle Copyrighted or Proprietary Materials Ethically

When implementing these trainings, it’s crucial to **respect copyright and not misuse union or proprietary course materials**. Many safety/leadership courses have copyrighted slides, videos, or handbooks. Here’s how to **share knowledge without infringing**:

- **Use public domain and openly available resources:** A lot of safety information is published for free by industry committees and government agencies. For example, the **Industry-Wide Labor-Management Safety Committee** has issued dozens of official **Safety Bulletins** on specific production hazards (from use of helicopters to mitigating water hazards). CSATF hosts these bulletins publicly on their site【2†L3-L11】 – you can download and distribute them since they are meant to be widely used on set. Incorporate these bulletins into your training handouts or slides. (They cover many topics the Safety Pass covers, in written guideline form.) Likewise, **OSHA regulations and guides** are public domain – if discussing things like ladder safety or hazard communication, you can quote OSHA guides or use OSHA’s training graphics (which are generally free use).

- **License or purchase content legitimately:** For any third-party courses (like FilmSkills, etc.), ensure you obtain the proper license. FilmSkills Academic, for example, has packages for schools – your space could subscribe to their safety series, which gives you legal access to their videos and materials to show your cohort【29†L64-L72】. If cost is an issue, reach out to the provider and explain your mission; they might offer a discount for a nonprofit or community group. **Do not simply copy union training manuals or videos without permission.** Not only is it unethical, it could also burn bridges with those organizations you want to partner with. If there’s a particular union manual you find invaluable (say, a Local 728 electrical safety pamphlet), ask the local if you can use excerpts or if they have a public version.

- **Invite speakers instead of copying slides:** One way to share proprietary knowledge without distributing the actual materials is to have someone who knows it come speak. For instance, the union’s **Harassment Prevention training** might use a specific video – you can instead invite a labor attorney or HR professional to deliver similar content in their own words. They’ll cover the same legal requirements (anti-harassment laws, reporting mechanisms) but with their own materials, avoiding any copyright issue. Similarly, instead of printing out a union’s “Safety Passport” textbook, have a veteran crew member talk through the key points and personally demonstrate equipment safety.

- **Develop your own training curriculum with original materials:** You can create presentations or handouts that *summarize* key guidelines in fresh words or visuals. As long as you’re not outright reproducing someone else’s copyrighted text or media, you’re in safe territory. For example, you might create a slideshow on “10 Golden Rules of Set Safety,” drawing from general principles (lock out tag out, never leave a weapon unattended, etc.) that are common knowledge in the industry. If you base it on multiple sources (union guidelines, OSHA, personal experience), it becomes an original compilation. **Attribute sources where appropriate** (especially for factual safety standards), but you won’t be violating rights by teaching common safety practices.

- **Provide references rather than copies:** In cases where certain definitive materials are proprietary (say, a detailed CSATF course booklet), provide your participants with references on where to find the info **instead of giving them the actual file**. For instance, you can say, “The union safety course covers XYZ – for further reading, see the Safety Bulletin on this topic【2†L3-L11】 or refer to [specific chapter] of the ANSI standards, etc.” This way, you acknowledge the source and direct people to obtain it through proper channels if they want.

- **Check usage permissions for any media:** If you use images or videos in your training, ensure they are either your own, Creative Commons licensed, or you have permission. It might be tempting to show a clip from an IATSE safety video – better to use a generic workplace safety video from YouTube that is allowed, or create a demonstration yourself. The same goes for DEI training content – many consulting firms have proprietary case studies or exercises; instead, utilize freely available case studies (like news articles about on-set incidents that you can discuss). For example, discussing the real-life safety incident that led to the “Safety for Sarah” movement (after camera assistant Sarah Jones’ tragic accident) is educational and doesn’t require using any copyrighted material – you can use news coverage as a case study to drive home safety awareness.

In short, **build your training library legally**. Plenty of material exists in the public domain or can be licensed. Not only will this keep you out of trouble, it also respects the unions and professionals who developed these trainings – which is important if you’re trying to partner with them. You want to be seen as an ally, not a content pirate.

You could reach out to Studio Arts or similar entities to learn how they became approved – or collaborate so your space becomes a satellite venue for WIOA-funded classes.
  - **State training funds (Employment Training Panel in CA)【18†L225-L233】【18†L231-L239】:** California’s ETP reimburses businesses for training their employees to upgrade skills. If your coworking space has an organizational structure (say you form a small production company for the space), and your “members” could be considered employees or contract workers, ETP might cover training costs up to 100%【18†L225-L233】. Other states have similar programs for industries they want to bolster (New York’s Empire State Development obviously invested in crew training). Check with your state film commission or labor department about training grants for film/media. The **California Film Commission’s Pilot Career Pathways Program** is one example – it took fees collected from productions and redirected them to fund community training partners for below-the-line jobs【26†L114-L122】. Keeping an eye on your state’s film incentive legislation can reveal such opportunities (e.g., next time your state updates a film tax credit, advocate that a portion be earmarked for training programs like yours).

- **Pursue philanthropic and arts grants:** Many foundations and arts councils fund programs in education, arts, and economic development:
  - **National Endowment for the Arts (NEA):** They occasionally fund media arts training initiatives, especially if there’s an artistic or community engagement angle.
  - **Local arts councils or film societies:** If your city has a film commission or arts council, propose your training program for a grant. They appreciate efforts that strengthen the local film ecosystem.
  - **Foundations focusing on diversity or youth:** Since your training includes DEI and leadership, you could appeal to foundations supporting social justice, youth empowerment, or workforce diversity. For instance, a foundation dedicated to empowering minorities in tech or media might fund your DEI workshops for filmmakers of color. Always connect the dots between what the funder cares about and what your program delivers (e.g., increased employment and representation in the creative industry).
  - **Education grants:** If you structure some trainings like a curriculum, you might qualify as an educational program. Grants that typically go to community colleges or vocational schools might extend to you via a partnership. Perhaps partner with a local community college’s film program – they can act as a fiscal sponsor or co-applicant for grants and you deliver the non-academic safety training that complements their program.

- **Use fiscal sponsorship to accept donations and grants:** If your coworking space is not a nonprofit, set up a **fiscal sponsorship** arrangement with a nonprofit organization. Groups like **Film Independent, From the Heart Productions, IDA, NW Film Forum, etc.** offer fiscal sponsorship programs for film projects and related initiatives【19†L1-L9】【19†L25-L33】. Under fiscal sponsorship, donations to your training program go through the nonprofit (making them tax-deductible for donors), and the nonprofit typically takes a small fee. This enables you to solicit **individual donations, crowdfunding, or foundation grants that require 501(c)(3) status**【19†L21-L30】. For example, you could run a crowdfunding campaign for “Free Safety Training for 100 Indie Filmmakers,” and have a sponsor like Film Independent collect the funds for your use. *Tip:* Choose a fiscal sponsor aligned with your mission – e.g., a local media arts center might be eager to sponsor you and even give support in kind (like space or marketing).

- **Seek scholarships or trainee stipends:** To lower barriers for participants, try to offer the training free or at minimal cost *to them*. You can achieve this by securing **scholarship funds or stipends**. The MediaMKRS/Reel Works loader training mentioned earlier actually paid a stipend to each trainee【7†L155-L163】 – funded by the sponsors. While you may not afford stipends initially, you can at least cover training materials or offer travel stipends if you get grant money. Also, if you get a corporate sponsor, see if they’ll fund a certain number of “seats” – e.g. a camera rental company might sponsor 5 scholarships for aspiring cinematographers in your safety program.

- **Leverage production incentives for training**: Some cities and states will give extra incentive dollars or grants to productions that do training. If your coworking space is attached to an indie production company that occasionally applies for grants or incentive programs, incorporate training into those budgets. For example, if a city’s film office offers a grant to local indie films, include a line item for crew training sessions (with a justification that it improves safety and quality). In some cases, productions are required to spend a bit on workforce development to qualify for certain funding – you can be the solution by providing the training.

**Action Step:** Develop a brief **funding plan** for your training program. Identify 3–5 potential funding sources (e.g., “City Arts Council grant – due April 1”, “Contact XYZ Studios CSR department in June”, “Apply for fiscal sponsorship with Film Independent”). For each, note what materials are needed (proposal, budget, etc.) and start the application process or outreach. Simultaneously, create a simple **budget for your training program** so you know how much money you need to run it sustainably (instructor fees, venue costs if any, online course licenses, etc.). This will help you make a compelling case to funders that every dollar will directly enhance filmmakers’ skills and safety.

## 6. Implement and Integrate Training in Your Coworking Space

With partnerships forming, resources gathered, and funding in progress, focus on **delivering the training effectively** within your coworking filmmaking space. Here are actionable steps and best practices, informed by successful examples:

- **Offer regular workshops and refreshers:** Treat safety, leadership, and DEI training as an ongoing part of your space’s culture – not one-off events. For example, you could institute a **monthly “Safety Sunday”** where one Sunday each month is dedicated to a 2-hour safety workshop (topics rotating through general safety, specific departments, etc.). Similarly, a **quarterly leadership roundtable** for project leaders and a **biannual DEI seminar** can be scheduled. Regularity ensures new members get trained soon after joining, and veterans get refreshers. (Even on union sets, crew must refresh certain courses every 3 years or so.)

- **Integrate training into productions and projects:** If your coworking space facilitates actual shoots (like members crew on each other’s short films), make training a *prerequisite or part of pre-production*. For example, before anyone starts filming in your shared studio, require that they attend a safety briefing or show proof of completed training. Some independent production collectives emulate union protocols: you can have a rule that *every project does a safety meeting on Day 1* and discusses things like emergency exits, nearest hospital, fire extinguisher locations, etc. Provide a **“Production Safety Packet”** to each project team – including a summary of safety guidelines (drawn from those bulletins and courses) and forms like accident report templates. By doing this, you **normalize the behavior** that training isn’t separate from filmmaking, but a part of it.

- **Measure and acknowledge completion:** Keep track of who has taken which training. Create a simple database or spreadsheet of your members and check off the sessions they’ve done. This helps identify who needs training and avoids anyone slipping through the cracks. Upon completing a core series (say your basic 3-part series: Safety 101, Leadership 101, DEI 101), **award them a certificate or even feature them**. For instance, announce in your newsletter or Slack channel congratulating the latest “Certified Safe Filmmakers” or something. It boosts buy-in – others will want that shout-out too. If you have the capacity, you might even build a small **ceremony or networking event at the end of a training cycle** to celebrate graduates – invite some industry guests, which can double as a mixer where funders or partners see the impact.

- **Ensure materials remain up-to-date:** The film industry is always evolving – new technology brings new safety concerns (drones, VR, etc.), and DEI standards shift. Assign someone (perhaps a volunteer committee of members) to **review training content annually**. They can check resources like the CSATF site for any new Safety Bulletins or updates (e.g. new bulletins on COVID-19 safety were issued in recent years). Also keep an ear out for industry news – if there’s an accident or incident in the news, consider incorporating the lessons learned into your next workshop. By staying current, you maintain credibility and effectiveness.

- **Foster a culture of mentorship and leadership-by-example:** Training isn’t just formal workshops. Encourage more experienced members to mentor newcomers in safe practices. For example, if someone who’s been through all your courses is producing a shoot with first-timers, prompt them to **assign a “safety captain” on set** or to do a mini-training on day one. This mirrors how union sets have department heads responsible for safety in their area. Also, if you have members who do join a union or take an official course, invite them to share what they learned with the group (without violating any test confidentiality, of course). This keeps knowledge flowing both directions – your training helped them get there, and now they bring back more insight.

- **Document and possibly publish your training model:** As you refine the program, you might write a short guide or case study on what you’re doing. This could be useful for applying to grants, and also helps others emulate you (which is good for the industry overall). For instance, **Creative BC publicly announced the launch and goals of their inclusive leadership training program【17†L187-L195】**, which helped spread awareness. You can do similarly on your website or social media: talk about the partnership with, say, Local 600 (if established) or the success stories of your trainees. Not only does this attract potential partners and funders, it also puts gentle pressure on the partners to keep supporting you (since their name is attached in a positive way).

## 7. Learn from Examples of Success

Finally, let’s look at **real-world examples of independent production spaces or organizations that have implemented training programs**. These illustrate what’s possible and provide a blueprint you can adapt:

- **Producers Guild’s Independent Production Safety Initiative (IPSI):** The PGA’s IPSI has helped numerous indie projects create safer sets. For example, indie producers who utilized the **PGA’s Set Etiquette (harassment prevention) training** reported improved on-set culture and confidence in handling issues. By making the training free and providing legal Q&A time, PGA removed the cost barrier and gave producers tools to prevent problems【15†L237-L245】. *Actionable Takeaway:* Even if you’re not PGA, you can mimic this by partnering with pro-bono lawyers or HR experts to offer similar harassment training and consultations for your indie teams.

- **Reel Works MediaMKRS Program (New York):** As mentioned, this partnership between a nonprofit and Local 600 created a pipeline for underrepresented New Yorkers to get into camera department jobs【7†L146-L154】. They combined technical training, safety, and on-set etiquette, and crucially, **paid trainees and cost nothing for them to attend**【7†L155-L163】. The support of Netflix and Empire State Development was key【7†L150-L158】. *Actionable Takeaway:* When approaching funders, highlight how programs like MediaMKRS are **building a talent pipeline while promoting diversity** – a win-win that your space can replicate on a smaller scale. Even without big funding at first, you can integrate mentorship and networking so trainees feel it’s a career stepping stone.

- **Independent film cooperatives with training elements:** Some community film centers inherently include training. For instance, **Northwest Film Forum (Seattle)** and **Austin Film Society** have historically run workshops for members (covering gear, safety basics, etc.) – often subsidized by grants or membership fees. Another example is **PhillyCAM (Philadelphia’s public access media center)** which provides certification courses on equipment and safety before members can use their gear. They ensure people learn studio safety and respect rules as part of membership. *Actionable Takeaway:* If your space has equipment or facilities to share, require a short training module to “get certified” to use them, like PhillyCAM does. It’s an organic way to enforce training – people want to use the gear, so they’ll take the safety class.

- **Actsafe’s “Safe on Set” initiatives (British Columbia):** Actsafe (the non-profit safety association in BC) has seen success in embedding a safety culture in indie productions. They note that many indie producers initially weren’t aware they had access to free safety support【34†L105-L113】. Through outreach (articles in indie production magazines, etc.), they got more productions coming to them for training and advice【34†L84-L92】【34†L91-L99】. *Actionable Takeaway:* Promotion and awareness matter. Once you have training resources, advertise them to the independent filmmaking community in your region. You might discover a pent-up demand. Actsafe’s model of being a go-to help desk for safety could inspire you to offer “office hours” where indie filmmakers can consult with your team on safety plans for their shoots.

- **California’s Pilot Skills Training (Career Pathways):** In its first run, this program funded organizations like Hollywood CPR and ManifestWorks to train folks who then got employed on real productions【26†L92-L100】【26†L98-L104】. The key to success was **pairing training with placement** – trainees weren’t left hanging; they had mentors and links to union jobs after training【26†L79-L87】【26†L81-L89】. *Actionable Takeaway:* If possible, connect your training graduates with work opportunities. Perhaps coordinate with local indie productions (or student films) to take on **trainees/interns in roles matching their training**. Even better, build relationships with production companies in your network who might hire PAs or crew – recommend your trained members for those jobs. The true measure of success is seeing your folks applying the safety and leadership skills on real sets.

By following the steps above and learning from these examples, **you can build a robust program that brings crucial Safety, Leadership, and DEI training into the independent filmmaking world**. You’ll be raising the professionalism of your community, preventing accidents and misconduct, and forging valuable connections with the broader industry. It’s a significant undertaking, but as the industry moves toward more inclusive and safer practices, your initiative can be at the forefront, demonstrating that indie filmmakers are just as committed to these standards as the big unions and studios.

Good luck, and remember that every workshop or certificate you implement is a step toward a healthier, more sustainable filmmaking environment for everyone!
