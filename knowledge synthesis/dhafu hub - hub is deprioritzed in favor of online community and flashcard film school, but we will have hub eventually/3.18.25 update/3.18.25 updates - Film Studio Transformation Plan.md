Simultaneously, explore partnering with a fiscal sponsor (an established nonprofit in the arts) so you can apply for arts and innovation **grants** to support these community activities. Grant funding could underwrite event costs or initial staffing without waiting for revenue from a space.  

## Roadmap 2: Pollution Studios MVP (Use Pollution as a Short-Term Proof of Concept)

**Goal:** Quickly activate Pollution Studios in its current form as a **minimum viable product** co-working space, while openly acknowledging and managing its shortcomings. This serves as a real-world pilot to test the concept of a film co-working stage.

**Description:** This roadmap treats *Pollution Studios as a temporary incubator*. With minimal changes, the team can start inviting a select group of filmmakers to use the space and validate the business model. The meeting already outlined an initial gameplan: a proof-of-concept phase involving rearranging furniture and creating functional zones for co-working, then testing with ~20–30 early adopters【18†L398-L406】. Following that plan, the space can be lightly reconfigured (e.g. set up co-working desks in the “PO Room”, create a lounge in the kitchen area) without heavy renovation. The emphasis is on speed – get the doors open for the community in a matter of weeks – and on learning – collect feedback on the layout, pricing, and usage. **Short-term fixes** will mitigate the big issues: for instance, install temporary roof patch solutions and schedule co-working hours around known noisy times (train schedules) to handle sound issues. Bonded internet should be set up right away to improve connectivity【7†L7-L15】. All of this demonstrates the concept’s value using the actual stage, which helps refine the idea and provides evidence (usage stats, member testimonials) for future investors or partners. However, it’s understood this is *not* a permanent solution; the team will concurrently plan for what happens after the MVP period (either convincing the landlord to fix issues, or transitioning members to a new location in the future).

**Next Steps (Pollution Studios MVP):**  
- **Secure Agreement with Owner:** Work with Asher (current manager) to formalize an arrangement for using Pollution Studios for co-working. This might be a short-term lease transfer, sublease, or even a handshake agreement if trust is high, but key is clarifying responsibilities (e.g. who handles any damages, how stage bookings by Asher or others will be coordinated so they don’t conflict with co-working hours).  
- **Space Preparations:** Execute the “quick wins” in the space: deep clean the facility, declutter unnecessary equipment, and rearrange existing furniture to create co-working sections as per the room-by-room plan. For example, convert the **“PO Room” into an open co-working area** with desks and comfortable chairs, set up the **client room as a quiet editing/post-production suite**, etc., as already envisioned. These changes require labor but little money. Fix any minor hazards. Set up a check-in system at the entrance and ensure basic amenities (coffee, Wi-Fi, printers) are ready.  
- **Mitigate Critical Issues:** Before members arrive, address the most disruptive issues to the extent possible. For the leaking roof, have buckets/tarps ready and avoid using/leasing the space on heavy rain days (communicate this clearly to members). For internet, implement the **bonded internet solution** (likely two cable lines combined) to get better upload speeds【7†L7-L15】 – coordinate with Spectrum or a third-party bonding service ASAP. For sound, provide noise-cancelling headphones to members and create an honest policy (e.g. “Quiet hours” when trains aren’t passing, or schedule audio-sensitive work in early mornings or late nights when the area is quieter). These measures show members you’re aware of the issues and actively managing them.  
- **Launch Pilot with Founding Members:** Invite the first cohort of, say, 20 filmmakers to start using the space as co-working members. These could be the **50 eager folks** identified earlier, narrowed down to those most ready to participate in a trial【20†L3355-L3362】. Offer them a discounted introductory membership or even a free trial period in exchange for feedback. Monitor how they use the space: which areas are most popular, what hours people come, and any complaints or suggestions they have. This user feedback will be invaluable for tweaking the model.

This will test the business model assumptions. For instance, is the stage still being rented ~10 days a month as hoped, and are co-working members causing any disruption to that or vice versa? Adjust offerings or pricing quickly if something’s not working.  
- **Plan for Transition:** Since we know Pollution Studios has **long-term risks**, use the MVP period (which might be a few months) to plan next steps. Two possibilities: (a) **If the concept is thriving**, use that momentum to negotiate with the landlord – perhaps propose taking over the lease entirely but only if the landlord fixes the roof, or negotiate a rent credit to fix it yourselves. This is ambitious given the landlord’s track record, but a conversation can be had if you have data showing a viable business (e.g. “we have 30 paying members and stage bookings, we can take a longer lease but need the roof addressed”). Or, more likely, (b) **prepare to relocate** after the pilot – start lining up alternative stage options (see Roadmaps 3 and 4) so the community you’ve built can migrate to a better long-term home. In either case, compile the success metrics from this MVP (membership numbers, revenue, testimonials, events hosted) into the **pitch deck for investors**. A proven concept in hand at even a small scale will greatly strengthen appeals for funding. Also, explore if any **grant funding** can support fixing immediate issues (though grants for capital repairs are tough, perhaps a safety or historic preservation grant if applicable, via a fiscal sponsor).  

## Roadmap 3: Quick Partnership Strategy (Pilot in a Different Stage ASAP)

**Goal:** Rapidly test the co-working space concept in a **partner’s film stage** to avoid the physical limitations of Pollution Studios. Essentially, find a willing studio owner from the list of 15 potential locations and use their stage as a temporary co-working site in a revenue-sharing or rental arrangement.

**Description:** This strategy recognizes that Pollution Studios’ building might be more trouble than it’s worth for proving the concept. Instead, identify another stage or warehouse that is underutilized and strike a deal to use it as a co-working hub on a trial basis. The 15 potential locations already identified serve as a starting pipeline – likely these are small studios or soundstages around the city that have availability. The approach is “quick” in that the goal is to secure an agreement with one of them within weeks, not months. By doing so, you bypass the roof, internet, and landlord issues for now, and can focus on community and operations. For example, if one of the potential stages has fiber internet or a better-maintained facility, the user experience would improve greatly. The partner stage benefits by getting rental income on days their stage would otherwise be dark (or a cut of membership fees), and you get a functional space without a long-term lease commitment. This lets you **demonstrate the co-working model in a real studio environment** with relatively low risk. If it doesn’t work out at that stage, both parties can part ways more easily than if you were locked into a lease – and you could then try another location. If it *does* work, this could even become a model of using **multiple partner stages** in different areas (as hinted in the meeting, the team envisioned eventually having stages in the Valley and West side as hubs【20†L3364-L3372】). In the short term, though, the priority is to pick one alternate stage and get the concept up and running there as a proof of concept outside Pollution Studios.

**Next Steps (Quick Partnership):**  
- **Filter the Candidate List:** Take the list of 15 potential stages and quickly evaluate which ones meet the minimum needs for a pilot. Key criteria might include: available floor space comparable to Pollution (enough for ~50 people and some set/storage areas), basic amenities (bathrooms, electricity, maybe a few office rooms), **good internet connectivity (ideally fiber or at least better upload speeds than 20 Mbps)**, and a reasonable location (accessible to your initial member group). Eliminate any that have obvious deal-breakers (e.g. if one stage is as infrastructure-poor as Pollution or if it’s too expensive). Aim to narrow it down to a handful of top choices (maybe 3–5).  
- **Outreach to Owners:** Contact the owners or managers of these short-listed stages immediately. Pitch the collaboration as a win-win: you have a group of filmmakers ready to pay for co-working space; if the stage is free on weekdays or during off-production times, it can generate income. Emphasize this is a **short-term trial** (for instance, a 3-month pilot), so they are not committing long-term and can revert to normal operations if it doesn’t work out. Be prepared with a basic proposal – for example, you handle managing the co-working members and day-to-day supervision, and the stage owner either charges a flat monthly rate or takes a percentage of membership fees. Keep it flexible and low-risk to entice a quick “yes.”  
- **Select a Partner & Formalize Terms:** Based on responses, select the stage that is most eager and suitable. Sign a simple MOU or short-term contract for using the space. Important details: what hours/days the co-working can operate (ensuring it doesn’t conflict with any film shoots that the stage books), what areas are accessible to members (parking, restrooms, kitchen, etc.), responsibility for utilities and cleaning, and insurance/liability coverage. Given the quick nature, aim for a **month-to-month agreement** or a pilot period after which both sides can review and extend.  
- **Port the Co-Working Setup to New Site:** Move quickly to set up the co-working environment in the partner’s stage. This could involve bringing over some portable equipment or furniture from Pollution Studios (if available and with Asher’s okay), or renting necessary tables/chairs. Implement the same concept: designated work areas, lounge area, maybe a small gear storage section if possible. Ensure the **tech infrastructure** is ready: test the internet at this location, set up Wi-Fi for members, etc. Because this stage might not be originally designed for co-working, address any gaps – e.g. if there’s no dedicated quiet room, perhaps bring in modular sound-dampening panels to create a semi-quiet corner for calls/editing. Essentially, treat it like a “pop-up” co-working space within an existing stage.  
- **Onboard Members & Run the Pilot:** Invite your initial community to use this new space. You might bring the same early adopters you lined up (some may be relieved to know you’ve found a location with better facilities). Introduce everyone to the partner stage’s rules and any differences. Then operate as usual – monitor usage, gather feedback, and troubleshoot issues that arise with the partner (e.g. if the stage suddenly needs a day for a shoot, have a plan B for members or coordinate a field trip that day). Keeping the partner happy is crucial, so maintain open communication.  
- **Assess and Iterate:** After a couple of months in the partner stage, sit down with the stage owner to evaluate. Did the arrangement work for both sides? Is the stage owner open to continuing or even expanding the partnership? Also assess member satisfaction: do they love the space? Any complaints about this location (maybe it’s great on all fronts, or maybe it has its own issues like less central location – note these). This will inform your next move. If it’s largely positive, you can either continue operating here while you plan a permanent home, or even consider formalizing a longer lease/partnership. If it’s not ideal, use what you learned to approach the next best stage on your list and refine the deal structure.  
- **Funding Angle:** Document the outcomes of the partnership pilot in terms of membership growth and revenues. If the concept proves itself in a higher-quality stage, that becomes a key selling point for raising funds. Update the **pitch deck** with comparisons: *“In a better-equipped facility, our membership grew to X and we achieved Y% of our revenue target, demonstrating the concept is sound when infrastructure isn’t a barrier.”* This will resonate with venture capital investors as proof that the only thing between you and scaling is securing the right space. Additionally, maintain the relationship with the partner stage – even if you move to get your own venue later, they could remain an ally or even invest in the venture if they see its success. Also consider leveraging a **fiscal sponsor** here too: some grant programs might support “adaptive reuse of underutilized spaces” or community innovation in the arts – a pilot like this could attract a small grant to offset rent, which you can pursue through a nonprofit intermediary.

## Roadmap 4: Deliberate Relocation Strategy (Find the Right Stage Before Full Launch)

**Goal:** Take a methodical approach to identify and secure the **optimal long-term location** for the co-working production space, even if it means delaying the launch. This is about **doing it right, not rushed** – evaluating multiple sites and choosing a stage that will serve the community for years without the major drawbacks currently faced.

**Description:** Instead of immediately using Pollution Studios or jumping on the first available alternative, this roadmap focuses on **strategic planning and due diligence**. The idea is to invest time upfront (several weeks or a few months) to explore different candidate facilities – including those 15 potential locations and others that might emerge – and rigorously compare them. Key factors to examine include structural condition (no major leaks!), availability of fiber internet or ability to install it, location centrality, parking capacity, acoustic isolation, and cost of lease. During this search period, the co-working concept’s rollout might be limited to community-building efforts (as in Roadmap 1) and perhaps very small-scale trials, but essentially you hold off on a full launch until the **best-fit stage** is secured. Once it is, you can move forward with confidence that the space won’t sabotage the business. This deliberate strategy reduces the risk of costly pivots later – you won’t have to uproot the community a second time due to unforeseen building problems because you will have vetted them. It also may give time to assemble funding so you can enter that new lease or purchase with sufficient capital for any needed build-out. The trade-off is a slower path to revenue and needing to keep people engaged while waiting. But if executed well, you launch strong out of the gate in a facility that can truly support the vision (perhaps even the first of multiple locations, setting the template for expansion).

**Next Steps (Deliberate Relocation):**  
- **Define “Ideal Space” Requirements:** Clearly list out what the project needs in a long-term home. For example: *10,000+ sq ft stage with at least two breakout rooms and outdoor area, reliable high-speed internet (fiber or equivalent), good electrical power supply, ceiling height for filming, minimal noise interference, <30 min from major filmmaker neighborhoods (Hollywood, Burbank, etc.), ample parking or adjacent parking lot, and affordable rent within budget.* Defining these criteria will guide your search and also prevent getting swayed by a place that might be available quickly but doesn’t actually meet the needs.  
- **Survey the Market:** Expand beyond the initial 15 if needed – engage a real estate broker who specializes in creative industrial spaces or use industry contacts to find out which stages or warehouses might be coming up for lease. Consider both traditional soundstages and unconventional spaces (an empty warehouse or big loft that could be converted). Compile a broad list of options that potentially match your criteria.  
- **Systematic Evaluation:** Create a comparison matrix (spreadsheet) for all candidate locations. Include factors like monthly cost, lease terms length, internet options available, known structural or neighborhood issues, any existing infrastructure (offices, AC, etc.), and landlord reputation (if known). If possible, **visit each promising site** with a trusted contractor or inspector to catch any red flags (e.g. roof age, plumbing, HVAC). This step is time-consuming but critical – it’s where you vet that the next space won’t have the same surprises as Pollution. Score or rank the sites based on how well they meet the requirements.  
- **Shortlist and Negotiate:** Identify the top one or two locations from your evaluation. Open discussions with those owners/landlords about your concept and needs. Negotiation points might include: ability to make alterations to create co-working zones, assurance that internet can be upgraded (perhaps the landlord would allow you to trench fiber or they might even split the cost if it increases property value), and flexibility on lease (maybe start with a 1-year lease with option to extend, or a few months free rent for improvements). Because you’re taking your time, you can negotiate firmly and walk away if terms aren’t right, knowing you have other options. Also use the extra time to line up finances – if a security deposit or upfront build-out money is required for the chosen space, ensure you have investors or grants ready (for instance, by now your **pitch deck** should be polished and you might have initial VC interest based on the community you’ve built).  
- **Pilot in the New Space (Soft Opening):** Once you secure the ideal location, do a controlled “soft launch.” Invite your early community members to start using the new facility in a beta phase. This gives a chance to test the space’s systems (does the internet truly hold up with many users? Is the parking sufficient on a busy day?). Because you did due diligence, there should be fewer issues, but real usage will surface any needed tweaks. Use this period to finalize any interior layout improvements and establish the workflows for running the space.  
- **Grand Launch and Scale:** After tuning the operation in the new space, officially launch the co-working production hub to the wider filmmaking community. At this point, you should feel confident in long-term operations here. You can now aggressively market memberships, plan larger events (knowing, for example, the roof won’t leak on your event!). Also consider a **grand opening grant** or local press — a fiscal sponsor could help get a small city arts grant to support an opening event or subsidize memberships for underrepresented filmmakers, which boosts your mission and publicity. With a stable home base, you can also revisit the expansion ideas (e.g. maybe keep talking to those other stage owners about future partnerships, or using Pollution Studios as a satellite if it’s still available, etc., but only as growth, not out of necessity). This deliberate approach sets a strong foundation, making the business more attractive to both investors and members due to reduced operational risks.

## Efficient Alternative Stage Search Recommendations

Finding the right stage efficiently requires a structured approach. Here’s how to streamline the search and decision process for an alternate location:

1. **Establish Key Criteria First:** Before contacting places, be clear on *must-haves* vs *nice-to-haves*. For example, a must-have might be *“adequate internet or ability to get it”* and *“no active roof leaks,”* whereas a nice-to-have could be *“existing office furniture.”* This prevents wasting time on stages that can’t work (e.g. a stage with no possible high-speed internet is a quick no-go). Write down these criteria and use them as a checklist for every location considered.

2. **Leverage the Existing List of 15:** Start with the 15 locations already identified. Gather any readily available info on each (from websites or prior knowledge): size, location, contact info, known amenities. Rank them roughly against your criteria to prioritize visits. However, remain open to other options – sometimes the perfect space isn’t on the initial list, so ask your network (and maybe the owners of the 15 if they know others) for additional leads.

3. **Initial Outreach & Info Gathering:** Reach out to each potential stage with a short list of questions that address your key criteria. For instance: *“Does your building currently have fiber internet or high-speed options? How is the roof condition? Any recent renovations? Is the space free during weekdays?”* The goal is to quickly eliminate spots that don’t meet needs (e.g. if someone says “no, we only have basic WiFi and we can’t upgrade,” you can likely drop that candidate). This step can be done via email or phone and saves time by narrowing the field before you schedule any tours.

4. **Site Visits and Evaluation:** For the locations that pass the initial screen, conduct in-person visits **as soon as possible**. Use a standardized evaluation form during each visit to rate things like: building condition (roof, AC, electrical), sound conditions (is it near a noisy highway or airport?), neighborhood safety and convenience, and landlord attitude. If you can, bring another colleague to each visit to catch details and discuss impressions right after. Taking photos and notes will help when comparing later.

5. **Compare Options Side by Side:** After visits, sit down and compare all viable options. It often helps to create a scorecard (for example, rating each location 1–5 on criteria like Internet, Infrastructure, Location, Cost, Landlord cooperation). This makes it easier to see which stage consistently scores highest across important factors. Pay attention not just to quantitative scores but also any “gut feeling” red flags – e.g. if a landlord seemed difficult in conversation, that might foreshadow future issues.

6. **Prioritize Quick Availability vs. Perfect Fit:** Decide what’s more important for your situation – speed or exact fit – and choose accordingly. If a particular stage meets 80% of criteria and is available *immediately*, it might be your pick for a quick pilot (Roadmap 3 style). If none check enough boxes, you might lean towards the deliberate search (Roadmap 4), which means continuing the hunt or negotiating to improve conditions at the top candidate. In either case, document why you chose that path (so you can explain to stakeholders and also revisit the decision if conditions change).

7. **Engage in Parallel Planning:** While searching, continue to **build the community and refine the business plan**. The search can take time with back-and-forth communications; use that time to keep growing your list of interested members (so demand is strong when you do find the space) and to update your financial projections based on real data you gather (like actual rent quotes, etc.). This ensures once you do find the right stage, you’re ready to hit the ground running on all fronts.

8. **Due Diligence Before Committing:** Once an alternate stage is selected (either for a quick pilot or long-term relocation), do one last round of due diligence. This might involve reviewing the lease agreement with a fine-tooth comb (or a lawyer) to spot any clauses about repairs or usage that could be problematic, checking references or talking to past tenants of that landlord if possible, and testing critical systems (take an engineer to test internet speed, for example). Verifying these details **before** signing will save headaches later and solidify that you’ve made the right choice.

By following these structured steps, the search for an alternative stage will be systematic and efficient, balancing speed with careful consideration. This way, whether you opt for a quick partnership trial or a deliberate permanent move, you’ll have confidence that the chosen space is the best available option for launching the film co-working and production hub.
