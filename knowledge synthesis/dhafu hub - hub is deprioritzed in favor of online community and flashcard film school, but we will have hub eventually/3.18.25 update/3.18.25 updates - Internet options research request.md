### Bonded Connections and SD-WAN (for Multi-Line Reliability)

At a business site, it’s common to use more than one internet connection for redundancy or bandwidth aggregation. Here’s how that could work at Grande Vista:

- **Dual ISP Redundancy:** The safest approach is to have two different ISPs – e.g., **AT&T fiber plus Spectrum coax**. This way, if one has an outage (fiber cut or cable issue), the other likely remains. Many businesses use a router that can failover automatically. With an SD-WAN setup or even basic load-balancing, you can actively use both links. For example, routine file uploads could go over the fiber (for speed) and guest Wi-Fi or less critical traffic over the cable, but if the fiber drops, all traffic can route through the cable (albeit with the cable’s upload limitation). SD-WAN appliances make this seamless; they can also do things like use both links at once for certain traffic (not quite doubling single-stream speed, but load-spreading multiple transfers).
- **Bonding for Throughput:** If one wanted to actually *combine* bandwidth (for instance, to get ~1.35 Gbps by adding a 1 Gbps fiber and a 350 Mbps cable together), some form of bonding or load balancing is needed. A true bonded connection (where two links act as one IP pipe) often requires a specialized provider or appliance that splits traffic at the packet level and reassembles it (like Peplink’s SpeedFusion or Cisco SD-WAN with multiple tunnels). It’s doable, and many SD-WAN services will achieve a similar outcome by balancing multiple parallel flows. For most business purposes, having multi-WAN load balancing yields effective bonding for things like multiple downloads or serving many users, even if a single large file transfer won’t exceed the speed of one link. 
- **ISP-Supported Bonding:** In some cases, ISPs themselves offer bonded solutions. AT&T could bond multiple T1s (legacy) or multiple DSL lines, but that’s not relevant here given fiber is available. No provider currently bonds two fiber GPON circuits into one service for small biz (they’d just sell a higher tier). Spectrum won’t bond two coax modems into one account (you’d have to get two accounts). Thus, bonding is really in the realm of the customer’s network or a managed service provider. We already mentioned XNET (bonding multiple cellular)【31†L5-L12】 – an analogous service exists for wired lines too (some companies offer a cloud bonding service where your traffic goes through their server which has connections to multiple ISPs).
- **SD-WAN Managed Service:** If the studio doesn’t have in-house IT to manage this, they can subscribe to a **Managed SD-WAN service**. AT&T offers one that can use multiple types of access (even AT&T will manage a third-party link as backup in some cases)【35†L1-L8】. Spectrum Enterprise’s SD-WAN can also handle a solution with, say, one fiber and one coax link combined【35†L9-L17】. These services usually involve a provider shipping an edge device to the site which handles the traffic and a monthly fee for the management. The benefit is they will handle configuring failover, QoS for voice, and so on, and provide a dashboard of performance. For a single-location business, SD-WAN is a bit heavy, but if this site is part of a larger network (e.g., a branch of a company or needs VPNs to other offices), it can be very useful.
- **Example Scenario:** The business could get **1 Gbps AT&T Fiber + 1 Gbps WeLink wireless**. Using both, they effectively have two separate gigabit paths – one wired, one wireless (so even a fiber cut affecting all wired ISPs wouldn’t down the wireless). With an SD-WAN, they could bond them such that uploads of video footage use both concurrently to send at 1+ Gbps aggregated, and interactive traffic is always on the lowest-latency path. This kind of setup approaches **99.99% uptime** reliability, comparable to a dedicated line with backup, but often at a lower cost than a single protected circuit from a telco. It’s just an illustration of how, given the multitude of options at this location, a savvy business can architect a very resilient network.
